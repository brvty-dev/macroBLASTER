What is macroBLASTER?

macroBLASTER is a free online tool that allows you to calculate a healthy, daily, macronutrient intake based on your personal attributes and the highly-regarded Harris-Benedict formula. It also includes safe adjustments for weight loss or weight gain should you choose either of these options.

What is the Harris-Benedict formula?

The Harris-Benedict equation was the result of a scientific study to determine an individual's basal metabolic rate (BMR), which is the number of calories a person requires each day to maintain their current weight.

The study was undertaken by James Arthur Harris and Francis Gano Benedict in 1919 and published by the Carnegie Institute of Washington. With improvements in technology, the equation was refined by Roza and Shizgal in 1984, and again by Mifflin and St Jeor in 1990. It is this most-recent version that macroBLASTER uses in its calculations.

How accurate is the Harris-Benedict formula?

The Harris-Benedict formula is one of the oldest and most accurate methods of calculating an individual's basal metabolic rate. The Mifflin-St Jeor revision is recommended by the US Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics.

Given the complexity and variety of human bodies, the equation is not perfect because it does not take a person's body composition into consideration. It will return the same result for someone who is very muscular as it would for someone who is overweight (providing they are the same age, height and weight).

The 1984 adjustment was considered to be 90% accurate for 95% of people. The 1990 update went further, identifying greater accuracy for normal-weight and overweight people, and less accuracy for underweight and obese people.

How do you determine the calories for weight loss and weight gain options?

Losing or gaining weight involves decreasing or increasing the calories you consume each day below or above your BMR (calculated by the Harris-Benedict formula).

There is a consensus that a deficit or surplus of approximately 500 calories is a safe and healthy limit for each of these approaches. If your goal is to lose weight, we subtract 500 calories from your BMR. If your goal is to gain weight, we add 500 calories to your BMR.

How do you determine activity levels?

Activity levels are based on the activity factor used by the Harris-Benedict formula, which calculates an individual's BMR. BMR is a theoretical figure that indicates a person's resting calory intake. In reality, people are not motionless all day, so the activity factor is a multiple applied to the BMR to take into consideration daily activity, such as cooking, going to the shops, or exercise.

Why is a healthy weight important?

A healthy weight is important because it supports body function and reduces the risk of many health problems, such as heart disease and high blood pressure. It also benefits day-to-day energy levels, mobility and mental health. People with a health weight tend to live longer.

Why would I want to gain weight?

Aside from addressing health issues that can cause an individual to lose weight, such as recent surgery or an eating disorder, the main reason an individual might want to gain weight is to build muscle mass and increase strength. This is usually undertaken by individuals who partake in sport and other physical activities, and will probably involve a resistance training regime.

Increased strength can improve athletic performance. Other benefits can include faster recovery from injury, and improved physical and mental health

What is the best way to lose weight?

Put simply, the best way to lose weight is by increasing your activity levels. You can find more information in our series of guides.

How is water intake determined and why is it important?

Drinking plenty of (non-alcoholic!) liquids, water in particular, will keep you hydrated. This is important because all our bodily functions, such as regulating temperature, pumping blood and digesting food, require water to work properly.

Generally, we need 6-8 glasses (1.5-2 litres) per day, but this can increase in hot weather or if you are more active. We also consume water in the foods we eat. The optimum amount for burning fat is roughly 1 fluid once for every kilogram of body weight. We use this figure and adjust it according to the activity level you select.

How do you calculate my macros?

Before we get into the maths, you need to know that every gram of protein or carbohydrates you eat gives your body 4 calories. Every gram of fat you eat gives you 9 calories.

Athletes and those who are familiar with their workout routine and fitness goals will tailor their macronutrients to suit their needs.

At macroBLASTER we have used some generalisations to get to the figures we provide. As a rule of thumb, when working out regularly, your body requires roughly 2 grams of protein for every kilogram you weigh (or 1 gram for every pound you weigh). About 20-30% of your calories should come from fat, and the rest from carbohydrates. With these assumptions and by knowing your BMR, we are able to work out specific ratios to use as a guide.

Those more confident in their workout regime may want to look into how they can adjust their macro intake accordingly. There are many books and articles available on the subject.
